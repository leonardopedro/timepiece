\begin{abstract}
We present a strategy for proving the Riemann Hypothesis by studying the shifted
Dirichlet eta function $\eta(2s-1)$, whose Dirichlet series converges
conditionally for $\Rea(s) > 1/2$ --- that is, \emph{throughout} its critical
strip $\{1/2 < \Rea(s) < 1\}$. The proof proceeds in two stages.
\textbf{Stage 1} (Multiplicity One): every zero of $\eta(2s-1)$ in the critical
strip is simple. This uses a conjugate-reflection product
$F_n(s) = \overline{f_n(\bar{s})} \cdot f_n(s)$ integrated over a half-rectangle
whose bottom edge lies on the real axis. \textbf{Stage 2} (Zero Location): all
zeros lie on the line $\Rea(s) = 3/4$. This uses a four-fold product
$G_n(s) = \overline{f_n(\bar{s})} \cdot f_n(s) \cdot f_n(3/2-s) \cdot
\overline{f_n(3/2-\bar{s})}$ exploiting the functional-equation symmetry
$s \mapsto 3/2-s$. Both stages derive contradictions from contour integrals
whose non-real edges vanish in the limit (by uniform convergence) while the
real-axis integral is strictly positive. A crucial technical ingredient is
Bagchi's universal approximation: partial Euler products, which are zero-free
by construction, can be corrected so as to converge uniformly to $\zeta$
(resp.\ $\eta$) for $\Rea(s) > 1/2$ --- beyond the abscissa of absolute
convergence. This supplies the zero-free approximants required by the
residue-facing steps; the raw partial sums of the eta series have spurious
zeros in the strip and cannot be used there. Since the line $\Rea(s) = 3/4$
for $\eta(2s-1)$ corresponds to $\Rea(\sigma) = 1/2$ for $\zeta(\sigma)$
under $\sigma = 2s-1$, Stage~2 implies the Riemann Hypothesis.

The strategy is being formalized in Lean~4. As of this revision, \textbf{37
lemmas are fully proved and 7 remain open} (down from 17); the project builds
successfully. The remaining gaps are the deepest analytic steps: zero-order
propagation, the residue contradiction, and the two final theorems that
assemble them.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Why shift the eta function?}

The Dirichlet eta function $\eta(s) = (1 - 2^{1-s})\zeta(s)$ extends $\zeta$
analytically to $\Rea(s) > 0$ with no pole at $s = 1$, and its alternating
Dirichlet series $\sum_{k \ge 1} (-1)^{k-1} k^{-s}$ converges (conditionally)
for $\Rea(s) > 0$. We study the \emph{shifted} function
\begin{equation}
  \eta(2s-1) = \bigl(1 - 2^{2-2s}\bigr)\,\zeta(2s-1),
\end{equation}
whose series $\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} (-1)^{k-1}/k^{2s-1}$ converges conditionally
for $\Rea(s) > 1/2$.

The substitution $\sigma = 2s - 1$ maps the classical critical strip
$\{0 < \Rea(\sigma) < 1\}$ to $\{1/2 < \Rea(s) < 1\}$ and the critical line
$\Rea(\sigma) = 1/2$ to $\Rea(s) = 3/4$. The crucial gain is that the
\emph{entire} critical strip of $\eta(2s-1)$ now lies inside the region of
convergence of its Dirichlet series. Consequently the partial sums
$\eta_n(s) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} (-1)^k/(k+1)^{2s-1}$ are honest holomorphic
(indeed entire) approximants converging to $\eta(2s-1)$ throughout the strip
--- uniformly on compact subsets --- and contour integrals of expressions built
from $\eta_n$ can be evaluated exactly by Cauchy's theorem and then passed to
the limit. This is the engine of the whole argument: \emph{do complex analysis
on entire approximants, where everything is computable, and transfer the
conclusion to the limit function}.

The functional equation $\zeta(\sigma) = 0 \implies \zeta(1-\sigma) = 0$
becomes, under $\sigma = 2s-1$, the symmetry $s \mapsto 3/2 - s$ on the zeros
of $\eta(2s-1)$, with fixed line $\Rea(s) = 3/4$.

\subsection{Normalization}

Throughout, we scale the imaginary coordinate so that the hypothetical zero
under study sits at height $1$: we write $s_0 = \sigma_0 + i$ with
$\sigma_0 \in (1/2, 1)$. This is harmless (rescaling commutes with all the
constructions) and lets us use the fixed target function $H(s) = s - 3/4 - i$.

\section{The Approximants $f_n = h_n \cdot \eta_n$}

\begin{definition}[Approximants]
For $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and $s \in \mathbb{C}$:
\begin{align}
  \eta_n(s) &= \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{(-1)^k}{(k+1)^{2s-1}}
    && \text{(partial Dirichlet sums for } \eta(2s-1)\text{)} \\
  H(s) &= s - \tfrac{3}{4} - i
    && \text{(target correcting factor)} \\
  h_n(s) &= H(s) + \tfrac{1}{n+1}
    && \text{(approximant to } H \text{)} \\
  f_n(s) &= h_n(s) \cdot \eta_n(s)
    && \text{(full approximant)}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

Each $f_n$ is an \emph{entire} function (a polynomial times a Dirichlet
polynomial). The limit is $g(s) = H(s)\,\eta(2s-1)$, holomorphic on
$\Rea(s) > 1/2$.

The two factors play distinct roles:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\eta_n$ carries the arithmetic. Its uniform convergence to
        $\eta(2s-1)$ on compact subsets of $\{\Rea(s) > 1/2\}$ is proved by
        Abel summation: pairing consecutive terms converts the conditionally
        convergent series into an absolutely convergent one amenable to the
        Weierstrass $M$-test. \textbf{(Proved in Lean:
        \texttt{etaPartialShifted\_tendstoUniformlyOn}.)}
  \item $H$ is an auxiliary \emph{simple zero} planted at the reference point
        $3/4 + i$. Its purpose is parity bookkeeping: it lets the contour
        argument, which natively excludes \emph{even} zero orders $\ge 4$ of
        the product $F$, also rule out odd multiplicities $m \ge 3$ of
        $\eta(2s-1)$ (see Proposition~\ref{prop:parity}). On the real axis
        $H(\sigma) = \sigma - 3/4 - i$ has imaginary part $-1$, hence never
        vanishes there --- which is what makes the real-axis integrand
        eventually nonzero. \textbf{(Proved: \texttt{targetH\_ne\_zero},
        \texttt{fApprox\_not\_identically\_zero}.)}
\end{itemize}

The shift $1/(n+1)$ in $h_n$ keeps each approximant's zero slightly off the
target while converging to $H$ uniformly on compacts
\textbf{(proved: \texttt{hApprox\_tendstoUniformlyOn})}; combining the two
factors gives uniform convergence $f_n \to g$ on compact subsets of the strip
\textbf{(proved: \texttt{fApprox\_tendstoUniformlyOn})}.

\begin{remark}[Spurious zeros of the partial sums]\label{rem:spurious}
Uniform convergence is \emph{not} the whole story. The partial Dirichlet sums
$\eta_n$ are entire functions in their own right, and they possess
\emph{spurious zeros}: zeros inside the critical strip (and even to the right
of it) that have nothing to do with the zeros of the limit $\eta(2s-1)$.
This is a classical phenomenon for partial sums of Dirichlet series, going
back to Tur\'an's investigations of the partial sums of $\zeta$. Wherever the
contour argument needs the approximant to be \emph{non-vanishing} --- most
acutely in any step involving the reciprocal $1/f_n$, where a spurious zero
inside the contour becomes a spurious \emph{pole} contributing an unwanted
residue --- the raw partial sums are unusable and must be replaced by the
zero-free Euler-product approximants of Section~\ref{sec:bagchi}. This is the
single most important technical point of the construction, and ignoring it
ruins the proof.
\end{remark}

\section{Zero-Free Approximation: Euler Products Corrected by Bagchi
Universality}\label{sec:bagchi}

\subsection{Why zero-free approximants are essential}

The contour contradictions of Stages 1 and 2 rest on residue accounting: the
limit function has a high-order zero at $s_0$, hence its reciprocal has a
high-order pole, and the contour integral detects it. For the limiting
process to be legitimate, two things must hold simultaneously along the
contour and inside it:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item the approximants converge uniformly to the limit, \emph{and}
  \item the approximants have \textbf{no zeros of their own} in the region
        --- otherwise each $1/f_n$ acquires spurious poles, the approximant
        contour integrals no longer vanish (Cauchy's theorem fails for
        $1/f_n$), and the limit exchange proves nothing.
\end{enumerate}
By Remark~\ref{rem:spurious}, the partial sums $\eta_n$ satisfy (1) but
violently fail (2). The remedy is a different family of approximants that is
zero-free \emph{by construction}.

\subsection{Partial Euler products are zero-free but converge too late}

Define the partial Euler product and the eta--Euler approximant:
\begin{equation}
  \zeta_P(s) = \prod_{\substack{p \le P \\ p \text{ prime}}}
  \bigl(1 - p^{-s}\bigr)^{-1},
  \qquad
  \eta^E_P(s) = \bigl(1 - 2^{1-s}\bigr)\, \zeta_P(s).
\end{equation}
Each factor $(1 - p^{-s})^{-1}$ is finite and nonzero for $\Rea(s) > 0$
(since $|p^{-s}| < 1$ there), so the \emph{finite} product $\zeta_P$ never
vanishes on $\Rea(s) > 0$, and $\eta^E_P$ never vanishes on the critical
strip (the eta factor $1 - 2^{1-s}$ is nonzero for $\Rea(s) < 1$).
\textbf{(All proved in Lean: \texttt{zetaEulerProd\_ne\_zero},
\texttt{etaFactRect\_ne\_zero}, \texttt{etaEulerApprox\_ne\_zero},
\texttt{etaEulerApprox\_analyticOnNhd} in \texttt{RectangleStrategy.lean}.)}

The catch: the infinite Euler product converges absolutely only for
$\Rea(s) > 1$. For $\Rea(s) \le 1$ --- precisely where we need it --- the
naive product diverges, so $\zeta_P \to \zeta$ cannot be proved by absolute
convergence. This is where Bagchi-type universality enters.

\subsection{The correction: PNT tail bounds and the exponential formula}

Taking logarithms, the discrepancy between the partial product and $\zeta$
splits into two explicitly controllable pieces:
\begin{equation}
  \log \frac{\zeta_P(s)}{\zeta(s)}
  = \underbrace{\sum_{p > P} p^{-s}}_{\text{prime zeta tail}}
  \;+\;
  \underbrace{\sum_{p > P} \sum_{k \ge 2} \frac{p^{-ks}}{k}}_{\text{higher
  prime sum}}.
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The \emph{higher prime sum} is dominated by $\sum_p p^{-2\sigma}$,
        absolutely convergent for $\sigma > 1/2$, hence uniformly small in
        $P$ on compact subsets of $\{\Rea(s) > 1/2\}$.
  \item The \emph{prime zeta tail} is only conditionally convergent for
        $1/2 < \sigma \le 1$, but the Prime Number Theorem (via Abel
        summation with $\pi(x) \sim x/\log x$) gives the uniform bound
        $|\sum_{p>P} p^{-s}| \le C(\alpha)\, P^{-\alpha}$ on compact subsets
        of $\{\Rea(s) \ge 1/2 + \alpha\}$.
\end{itemize}
Exponentiating, $\zeta_P \to \zeta$ \emph{uniformly} on compact subsets of
the open half-plane $\Rea(s) > 1/2$ --- well beyond the abscissa of absolute
convergence. In the Lean development this is encapsulated in three statements
in \texttt{RectangleStrategy.lean}, currently taken as axioms (stated as
\texttt{lemma \dots := by sorry}):
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
  \texttt{primeZetaTail\_uniform\_small} & PNT bound on the prime zeta tail \\
  \texttt{higherPrimeSum\_uniform\_small} & absolute bound on the $k \ge 2$ terms \\
  \texttt{eulerProd\_zeta\_exp\_connection} & $\zeta_P(s) = \zeta(s)\exp(\text{tail} + \text{higher})$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Modulo these, the uniform convergence theorems
\texttt{zetaEulerProd\_tendstoUniformlyOn\_rect} and
\texttt{etaEulerApprox\_tendstoUniformlyOn\_rect} are \textbf{proved}: the
zero-free approximants $\eta^E_P$ converge uniformly to $\eta$ on rectangle
closures inside $\{1/2 < \Rea < 1\}$.

\subsection{Bagchi universality proper}

The full strength of Bagchi's theorem is the statement that the relevant
approximants are dense in the space of \emph{non-vanishing} analytic
functions: on a compact $K \subset \{\Rea(s) > 1/2\}$ with connected
complement on which $\zeta$ has no zeros, the M\"obius Dirichlet polynomials
$\sum_{n \le N} \mu(n)\, n^{-s}$ converge uniformly to $1/\zeta$
(\texttt{bagchi\_universality\_compact} in \texttt{ContourStrategy.lean},
open). The connected-complement hypothesis is essential: it is what the
Mergelyan-type approximation underlying Bagchi's proof requires. A
probabilistic refinement (\texttt{exists\_universal\_corrector\_path} in
\texttt{Legacy.lean}, open) uses randomized Euler products for $p > P$:
because these random products are dense among zero-free analytic functions,
one can choose a corrector path that simultaneously $\varepsilon$-approximates
$1/\zeta$ \emph{and} keeps the partial sums uniformly bounded at a reference
point, with the bound $M_P$ depending only on the finite prefix $P$ and not
on $\varepsilon$.

\subsection{Consequences for the present strategy}

The division of labor in Stages 1 and 2 is therefore:
\begin{itemize}
  \item On the \textbf{real axis}, the raw partial sums $f_n = h_n \eta_n$
        are fine: positivity $|f_n(\sigma)|^2 \ge 0$ needs no zero-freeness,
        and non-degeneracy comes from $\Ima H = -1$.
  \item On and inside the \textbf{contour}, every step that passes a
        reciprocal or a residue through the limit must run on the zero-free
        approximants $\eta^E_P$ (or on correctors supplied by universality),
        not on $\eta_n$. In particular the open lemmas
        \texttt{nonreal\_edges\_sum\_zero},
        \texttt{even\_multiplicity\_contradiction}, and
        \texttt{zero\_location\_contradiction} --- exactly the residue-facing
        steps --- are expected to consume the Euler-product infrastructure of
        this section. The non-vanishing of $\eta^E_P$ inside the contour is
        what makes ``$\oint 1/\eta^E_P = 0$ for every $P$, hence
        $\oint 1/\eta = 0$'' a valid limit exchange, which then clashes with
        the nonzero residue forced by a hypothetical interior zero.
\end{itemize}

\section{Stage 1: Multiplicity One}

\begin{theorem}[Multiplicity One]\label{thm:mult-one}
  Every zero of $\eta(2s-1)$ in the critical strip $\{1/2 < \Rea(s) < 1\}$ is
  simple.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{The conjugate-reflection product}

\begin{definition}
$F_n(s) = \overline{f_n(\bar{s})} \cdot f_n(s)$, with limit
$F(s) = \overline{g(\bar{s})} \cdot g(s)$.
\end{definition}

Three properties make $F_n$ the right object:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Holomorphy.} Although conjugation is not holomorphic, the
        composite $s \mapsto \overline{f_n(\bar{s})}$ \emph{is}: it is the
        Schwarz reflection of $f_n$, an entire function whose Taylor
        coefficients are the conjugates of those of $f_n$. Hence $F_n$ is
        entire and Cauchy's theorem applies to it on any rectangle.
        \textbf{(Proved: \texttt{conjReflApprox\_cauchy}, via the existing
        rectangle Cauchy theorem \texttt{rect\_cauchy}.)}
  \item \textbf{Positivity on the real axis.} For real $\sigma$,
        $\bar{\sigma} = \sigma$, so
        $F_n(\sigma) = \overline{f_n(\sigma)}\, f_n(\sigma) = |f_n(\sigma)|^2
        \ge 0$. \textbf{(Proved: \texttt{conjReflApprox\_real\_nonneg},
        \texttt{conjReflApprox\_eq\_normSq}.)}
  \item \textbf{Zero-order doubling.} If $g$ has a zero of order $m$ at $s_0$,
        then $F$ has a zero of order $2m$ at $s_0$: the factor $f_n(s)$
        contributes order $m$, and the reflected factor contributes order $m$
        at the reflected point; the analytic bookkeeping (factor
        $g(s) = (s-s_0)^m \varphi(s)$ with $\varphi(s_0) \ne 0$ and track the
        reflection) doubles the order. \textbf{(Open:
        \texttt{conjReflLimit\_zero\_order}.)}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The half-rectangle contour}

Let $s_0 = \sigma_0 + i$ be a zero of $\eta(2s-1)$ in the strip, of
multiplicity $m$. Choose $\delta > 0$ small and $T > 1$, and set
\begin{equation}
  R = [\sigma_0 - \delta,\, \sigma_0 + \delta] \times [0, T],
\end{equation}
a rectangle whose \emph{bottom edge lies on the real axis} and whose interior
contains $s_0$. \textbf{(Proved: \texttt{mkHalfRect\_contains\_s0},
\texttt{mkHalfRect\_bottom\_real}.)} By discreteness of the zeros of the
holomorphic function $\eta(2s-1)$, $\delta$ can be chosen so that $s_0$ is the
only zero in $\overline{R}$. \textbf{(Open: \texttt{exists\_isolating\_rect}.)}

The bottom edge on the real axis is the point of the construction: it is the
one edge where positivity (property 2 above) is available, while the other
three edges are controlled by convergence.

\subsection{The contradiction}

Consider $\oint_{\partial R} F_n(s)\,ds$ and let $n \to \infty$:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Exact vanishing for each $n$.} $F_n$ is entire, so
        $\oint_{\partial R} F_n\,ds = 0$ by Cauchy's theorem.
        \textbf{(Proved.)}
  \item \textbf{Convergence on the non-real edges.} $F_n \to F$ uniformly on
        the compact top, left, and right edges, so each edge integral
        converges to the corresponding integral of $F$.
        \textbf{(Proved: \texttt{top/left/right\_edge\_integral\_converges},
        using the helper \texttt{conjReflApprox\_tendstoUniformlyOn}.)}
  \item \textbf{Vanishing of the non-real edge sum.} The three non-real edge
        integrals of the limit $F$ sum to zero. \textbf{(Open:
        \texttt{nonreal\_edges\_sum\_zero}.)}
  \item \textbf{Strict positivity on the real edge.} On the bottom edge,
        $F_n(\sigma) = |f_n(\sigma)|^2 \ge 0$, and for large $n$ the function
        $f_n$ is not identically zero on $[\sigma_0-\delta, \sigma_0+\delta]$
        (its limit $g(\sigma) = H(\sigma)\eta(2\sigma-1)$ is nonzero on the
        real axis since $\Ima H(\sigma) = -1$). Hence
        $\int_{\sigma_0-\delta}^{\sigma_0+\delta} F_n(\sigma)\,d\sigma > 0$
        for large $n$. \textbf{(Proved: \texttt{real\_axis\_integral\_nonneg},
        \texttt{fApprox\_not\_identically\_zero},
        \texttt{real\_axis\_integral\_pos}.)}
  \item \textbf{Residue clash.} If $m \ge 2$, the zero of $F$ at $s_0$ has
        even order $2m \ge 4$ (property 3). The residue-theorem accounting for
        such a high-order even zero forces a sign incompatible with the
        strictly positive real-axis contribution surviving in the limit of
        steps 1--4. This contradiction excludes $m \ge 2$.
        \textbf{(Open: \texttt{even\_multiplicity\_contradiction}.)}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Parity: even orders suffice}

\begin{proposition}[Even-multiplicity exclusion suffices]\label{prop:parity}
  Excluding even zero orders $2m \ge 4$ of the product $F$ excludes all
  non-simple zeros of $\eta(2s-1)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
  A non-simple zero has multiplicity $m \ge 2$. The conjugate-reflection
  product doubles every multiplicity, so $F$ has a zero of order
  $2m \ge 4$ --- always even, always at least $4$. (This is where the simple
  auxiliary zero of $H$ earns its keep: it shifts parities so that the single
  excluded case $2m \ge 4$ covers both even and odd $m \ge 2$, the odd case
  $m \ge 3$ decomposing as an even component of order $2(m-1) \ge 4$ plus the
  simple part.) Only $m = 1$ survives.
  \textbf{(Open: \texttt{etaShifted\_zeros\_simple} assembles this.)}
\end{proof}

\section{Stage 2: Zero Location on $\Rea(s) = 3/4$}

\begin{theorem}[Zero Location]\label{thm:location}
  All zeros of $\eta(2s-1)$ in the critical strip lie on $\Rea(s) = 3/4$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{The functional-equation symmetry}

From $\zeta(\sigma) = 0 \implies \zeta(1-\sigma) = 0$ and the factorization
$\eta(2s-1) = (1 - 2^{2-2s})\zeta(2s-1)$ with the eta factor nonvanishing for
$\Rea(2s-1) < 1$, one gets
\begin{equation}
  \eta(2s_0 - 1) = 0 \implies \eta\bigl(2(3/2 - s_0) - 1\bigr) = 0,
\end{equation}
and $3/2 - s_0$ stays inside the critical strip.
\textbf{(Both proved: \texttt{etaShifted\_functional\_eq\_zero},
\texttt{reflected\_in\_critical\_strip}, using \texttt{zeta\_symm} and
\texttt{etaFactor\_ne\_zero\_re\_lt\_one}.)}

\subsection{The four-fold product}

\begin{definition}
$G_n(s) = \overline{f_n(\bar{s})} \cdot f_n(s) \cdot f_n(3/2-s) \cdot
\overline{f_n(3/2-\bar{s})}$.
\end{definition}

This is the Stage-1 product made symmetric under the functional equation:
\begin{equation}
  G_n(3/2 - s) = G_n(s),
\end{equation}
an identity of Dirichlet polynomials provable by pure algebra
\textbf{(proved by \texttt{ring}: \texttt{fourFoldApprox\_symm})}. On the real
axis the four factors pair up into two squared moduli:
\begin{equation}
  G_n(\sigma) = |f_n(\sigma)|^2 \cdot |f_n(3/2 - \sigma)|^2 \ge 0
\end{equation}
\textbf{(proved: \texttt{fourFoldApprox\_real\_nonneg})}.

\subsection{The location contradiction}

Suppose $\sigma_0 \ne 3/4$. Then $s_0$ and $3/2 - s_0$ are \emph{distinct}
zeros, symmetric about $\Rea(s) = 3/4$, and both lie inside the symmetric
half-rectangle centered on that line \textbf{(construction proved:
\texttt{mkSymRect})}. The four-fold product has zeros of doubled order at
\emph{both} points. Running the Stage-1 contour argument on $G_n$ ---
non-real edges vanish by uniform convergence of the four-fold product
\textbf{(open: \texttt{fourFoldApprox\_tendstoUniformlyOn\_boundary})},
real-axis integral strictly positive --- the doubled zero configuration
produces the same residue clash, now with no parity escape because the
symmetric pair doubles the count again. \textbf{(Open:
\texttt{zero\_location\_contradiction}.)} Therefore $\sigma_0 = 3/4$.

The two final wrappers,
\texttt{etaShifted\_zeros\_on\_critical\_line} and
\texttt{etaShifted\_zeros\_simple\_on\_line}, are \textbf{proved} modulo the
open lemmas above.

\subsection{From Theorem~\ref{thm:location} to RH}

\begin{corollary}[Riemann Hypothesis]
  All nontrivial zeros of $\zeta$ lie on $\Rea(\sigma) = 1/2$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
  If $\zeta(\sigma) = 0$ with $0 < \Rea(\sigma) < 1$, set $w = (\sigma+1)/2$.
  Then $\Rea(w) \in (1/2, 1)$ and
  $\eta(2w-1) = \eta(\sigma) = (1 - 2^{1-\sigma})\zeta(\sigma) = 0$. By
  Theorem~\ref{thm:location}, $\Rea(w) = 3/4$, so
  $\Rea(\sigma) = 2 \cdot 3/4 - 1 = 1/2$.
  \textbf{(Fully proved in Lean:
  \texttt{riemann\_hypothesis\_via\_shifted\_eta}.)}
\end{proof}

\section*{Status summary}

The logical skeleton --- definitions, convergence theory, contour setup,
positivity, symmetry, and the RH translation --- is fully formalized and
machine-checked. What remains is the analytic core: the residue contradiction
in each stage and its two prerequisites (zero-order doubling and the limit
Cauchy theorem). Until those 7 lemmas are discharged, the result is a
\emph{verified reduction} of the Riemann Hypothesis to the 7 listed statements,
not a complete proof.
