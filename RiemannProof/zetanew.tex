\begin{abstract}
We present a strategy for proving the Riemann Hypothesis that works
\emph{directly} with $\zeta$ on its critical strip $\{0 < \Rea(s) < 1\}$, with no
auxiliary shift. The idea is a single contour identity: if $\eta(s) = (1 -
2^{1-s})\zeta(s)$ had a zero $s_0$ with $1/2 < \Rea(s_0) < 1$, enclose it in a
contour that \emph{straddles} the line $\Rea(s) = 1$ --- reaching from
$\Rea = 1/2 + \delta$ on the left to $\Rea > 1$ on the right --- and derive a
contradiction between $\oint 1/\eta = 0$ (Cauchy) and $\oint 1/\eta \ne 0$
(residue theorem at the interior zero). Because the poles of $1/\eta$ are
isolated, the contour need not be a rectangle: it is shaped to enclose each
critical-strip zero individually --- \textbf{one contour integral per zero} ---
while leaving the eta-factor zeros on the line $\Rea(s) = 1$ safely outside.

The engine that makes the Cauchy side legitimate is a new family of
approximants requested for this revision: a \textbf{random, exponential} Euler
product
\[
  \eulerG_P^{v}(s,\omega) \;=\;
  \exp\!\Bigl(\underbrace{\textstyle\sum_{p \le P} X_p(\omega)\, p^{-s}}_{\text{Gaussian Dirichlet sum}}
  \;+\; \underbrace{\log \eta^E_P(s)}_{\text{Bagchi centering}}\Bigr),
  \qquad X_p(\omega) = \sqrt{v}\,(g_p^{\mathrm{re}} + i\,g_p^{\mathrm{im}}),
\]
with independent complex Gaussian coefficients of variance $v$. Two structural
gains follow at once. First, $\eulerG$ is an exponential, hence \textbf{never
zero with no hypothesis on $s$}; so $1/\eulerG$ is entire wherever the centering
is analytic and $\oint_{\partial R} 1/\eulerG = 0$ holds \emph{for free}, with no
separate non-vanishing proof. Second, $\log \eulerG$ is linear in the Gaussians,
so $\operatorname{Var}(\log \eulerG) = v \sum_{p \le P} p^{-2\Rea(s)}$ is
proportional to $v$: small variance concentrates $\eulerG$ on its mean $\eta^E_P$
and gives uniform approximation. Crucially, the exponent is \emph{linear} in the
prime terms $p^{-s}$ (primes only, no prime-power corrections), so a
\textbf{single} random Dirichlet series carries the approximant all the way to
$\eta$: the primes-only Gaussian-2D ensemble, restricted to the realizations that
converge locally uniformly on $\{\Rea(s) > 1\}$, is already universal. This
replaces the earlier two-term correction (prime-zeta tail \emph{plus}
higher-prime-power sum) by one series.

The strategy is being formalized in Lean~4 in
\texttt{RiemannProof/GaussianEuler.lean}, now wired into the build root. The
structural and assembly layer is fully machine-checked; the analytic core is
isolated into \textbf{six clearly-marked \texttt{sorry}s}, of which the
load-bearing one is a single universality input --- the primes-only Gaussian-2D
series --- and the rest are an elementary Euler-product $M$-test, a contour
construction, and routine reciprocal/limit assembly. The result is at present a
\emph{verified reduction} of RH to those six statements, not a complete proof.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The rectangle strategy}

Write $\eta(s) = (1 - 2^{1-s})\zeta(s)$ for the Dirichlet eta function, which
extends $\zeta$ to $\Rea(s) > 0$. Since the eta factor $1 - 2^{1-s}$ is nonzero
for $\Rea(s) < 1$, the zeros of $\eta$ in the open critical strip coincide
exactly with those of $\zeta$. The Riemann Hypothesis is the statement that none
of them lie off the line $\Rea(s) = 1/2$, i.e.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:goal}
  1/2 < \Rea(s_0) < 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; \zeta(s_0) \ne 0.
\end{equation}

Suppose, for contradiction, $\zeta(s_0) = 0$ with $1/2 < \Rea(s_0) < 1$. Choose a
small $\delta > 0$ and enclose $s_0$ in a positively-oriented contour $\Gamma$
that reaches from $\Rea = 1/2 + \delta$ on the left to some $\Rea = x_{\mathrm{hi}}
> 1$ on the right --- so that $\Gamma$ \emph{straddles} the vertical line
$\Rea(s) = 1$ --- and whose interior contains $s_0$ as its \emph{only} zero of
$\eta$. The simplest such $\Gamma$ is the axis-parallel rectangle
\begin{equation}
  R = [\tfrac12 + \delta,\; x_{\mathrm{hi}}] \times [y_{\mathrm{lo}}, y_{\mathrm{hi}}],
  \qquad x_{\mathrm{hi}} > 1,\quad s_0 \in \operatorname{int} R;
\end{equation}
when a rectangle would enclose an eta-factor zero on $\Rea = 1$ it is replaced by
a small indentation of the same rectangle (Section~\ref{sec:etafac}). The
contradiction is the clash of two evaluations of one contour integral:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cauchy side:} $\displaystyle\oint_{\partial R}\frac{ds}{\eta(s)} = 0$,
        obtained as the limit of $\oint_{\partial R} 1/\eulerG = 0$ over the
        never-vanishing approximants;
  \item \textbf{Residue side:} $\displaystyle\oint_{\partial R}\frac{ds}{\eta(s)} \ne 0$,
        because the interior zero $s_0$ makes $1/\eta$ have a pole with nonzero
        winding contribution.
\end{itemize}
The two are incompatible, so no such $s_0$ exists and \eqref{eq:goal} holds.
This is structurally far simpler than the earlier conjugate-reflection
strategy: there is no auxiliary planted zero, no Schwarz reflection, no parity
bookkeeping, and no four-fold symmetric product --- just one rectangle and one
reciprocal.

\subsection{Why the straddle, and the two convergence regimes}

The rectangle deliberately crosses $\Rea(s) = 1$ because the two halves are
controlled by completely different analytic mechanisms, and the straddle lets the
easy half do as much work as possible.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Right edge ($\Rea(s) \ge a > 1$): absolute convergence.} Here the
        ordinary Euler product $\prod_{p \le P}(1 - p^{-s})^{-1}$ converges to
        $\zeta$ uniformly by a Weierstrass $M$-test against $\sum_n n^{-a}$. No
        randomness is needed ($v = 0$ suffices) and no deep input is invoked.
  \item \textbf{Three left edges ($\Rea(s) > 1/2$): universality / PNT skeleton.}
        On the left, bottom, and top edges --- a compact ``U''-shaped arc whose
        complement is connected --- the bare Euler product diverges (the
        prime-zeta tail $\sum_{p > P} p^{-s}$ does not converge for
        $\Rea(s) \le 1$). Uniform approximation of $\eta$ here is the Voronin/Bagchi
        universality regime, and this is where the Gaussian correction enters.
\end{itemize}
The connected-complement geometry of the three-edge arc is exactly the hypothesis
that Mergelyan-type approximation (the heart of Bagchi's proof of universality)
requires.

\section{The Gaussian Exponential Euler Product}

\subsection{The model}

Fix a cutoff $P$, a variance scale $v \ge 0$, and a sample point $\omega$ in the
space $\Omega = \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}$ recording, for each
prime index, the real and imaginary parts of a Gaussian.

\begin{definition}[Gaussian Euler product]\label{def:eulerG}
With $\eta^E_P(s) = (1 - 2^{1-s})\,\prod_{p \le P}(1 - p^{-s})^{-1}$ the
deterministic eta--Euler approximant, set
\begin{align}
  X_p(\omega) &= \sqrt{v}\,\bigl((\omega_p)_1 + i\,(\omega_p)_2\bigr)
    && \text{(complex Gaussian coefficient, variance $v$)} \\
  \texttt{gaussSum}(s,\omega) &= \sum_{\substack{p \le P\\ p\ \mathrm{prime}}} X_p(\omega)\, p^{-s}
    && \text{(finite random Dirichlet sum)} \\
  \texttt{cCorr}(s) &= \log \eta^E_P(s)
    && \text{(deterministic Bagchi centering)} \\
  \eulerG_P^{v}(s,\omega) &= \exp\bigl(\texttt{gaussSum}(s,\omega) + \texttt{cCorr}(s)\bigr).
\end{align}
\end{definition}

In Lean these are \texttt{Xg}, \texttt{gaussSum}, \texttt{cCorr},
\texttt{logEulerG}, and \texttt{eulerG} in \texttt{GaussianEuler.lean}. The
centering is chosen so that $\exp(\texttt{cCorr}(s)) = \eta^E_P(s)$ on the strip
\textbf{(proved: \texttt{exp\_cCorr\_eq\_etaEulerApprox})}, whence the
factorization
\begin{equation}\label{eq:factor}
  \eulerG_P^{v}(s,\omega) = \exp\bigl(\texttt{gaussSum}(s,\omega)\bigr)\cdot \eta^E_P(s)
  \qquad (\tfrac12 < \Rea(s) < 1)
\end{equation}
\textbf{(proved: \texttt{eulerG\_eq\_mul})}. At $v = 0$ all coefficients vanish,
$\texttt{gaussSum} = 0$, and $\eulerG_P^{0} = \eta^E_P$ wherever the latter is
nonzero \textbf{(proved: \texttt{eulerG\_zero\_eq\_etaEulerApprox} and its
$\Rea > 1$ form \texttt{eulerG\_zero\_eq\_etaEulerApprox\_gt\_one})}.

\begin{remark}[A single correction series]\label{rem:onecorrection}
The exponent $\texttt{gaussSum}$ is \emph{linear} in the prime terms $p^{-s}$,
with no prime-power ($k \ge 2$) terms: it is the logarithm of a product of
Gaussian factors $\exp(X_p\, p^{-s})$, not of $(1 - X_p\, p^{-s})^{-1}$.
Consequently the correction that carries the approximant from the partial Euler
product across $\Rea(s) = 1$ to $\eta$ is a \textbf{single} random Dirichlet
series over primes. Universality holds for this primes-only, Gaussian-2D ensemble
once one restricts to the realizations $\omega$ for which
$\sum_p X_p(\omega)\, p^{-s}$ converges locally uniformly on $\{\Rea(s) > 1\}$:
on that subspace the family $\{\exp(\sum_p X_p\, p^{-s})\}$ is dense among the
zero-free holomorphic functions on compacts of the strip with connected
complement. This is a structural simplification of the deterministic
Euler-product expansion, whose logarithm splits into \emph{two} pieces ---
\[
  \log\frac{\zeta_P(s)}{\zeta(s)}
  = \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_{p > P} p^{-s}}_{\text{prime-zeta tail}}
  + \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_{p > P}\sum_{k \ge 2}\frac{p^{-ks}}{k}}_{\text{higher prime powers}} .
\]
The Gaussian model needs only the first (the linear prime series); the
higher-prime-power sum, and its separate bound, drop out of the analytic debt
(\S\ref{sec:status}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{The structural payoff}

\begin{lemma}[Never zero]\label{lem:nonzero}
$\eulerG_P^{v}(s,\omega) \ne 0$ for all $P, v, s, \omega$ --- with no hypothesis
whatsoever on $s$. \textbf{(Proved: \texttt{eulerG\_ne\_zero}, one line: an
exponential is never zero.)}
\end{lemma}

This is the entire reason for the exponential form. In the old multiplicative
approximant $\prod(1 - X_p p^{-s})^{-1}$ one had to prove, separately and
painfully, that the approximant has no spurious zeros inside the contour
(Tur\'an's spurious-zero phenomenon); otherwise $1/\eulerG$ would acquire
spurious poles and Cauchy's theorem for the reciprocal would fail. With the
exponential form that obligation simply does not arise.

\begin{lemma}[Free Cauchy vanishing]\label{lem:cauchy}
On any rectangle $R$ on whose closure $\eta^E_P$ is analytic and nonzero,
$\eulerG_P^{v}(\cdot,\omega)$ is analytic and nonzero, so
\[
  \oint_{\partial R}\frac{ds}{\eulerG_P^{v}(s,\omega)} = 0.
\]
\textbf{(Proved: \texttt{eulerG\_differentiableOn},
\texttt{recipEulerG\_boundaryIntegral\_eq\_zero}, via the rectangle Cauchy theorem
\texttt{rect\_cauchy}.)}
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Variance is linear in $v$]\label{lem:variance}
Because $\log \eulerG = \texttt{gaussSum} + \texttt{cCorr}$ is an affine function
of the independent Gaussians $\{X_p\}$,
\[
  \operatorname{Var}\bigl(\log \eulerG_P^{v}(s,\cdot)\bigr) = v \sum_{p \le P} |p^{-s}|^2 = v\sum_{p \le P} p^{-2\Rea(s)}.
\]
Hence taking $v \downarrow 0$ concentrates $\eulerG$ on its mean $\eta^E_P$, and a
Gaussian-net plus Chebyshev argument upgrades this to uniform closeness on a
compact arc.
\end{lemma}

\begin{remark}[Where the variance must stay finite]
The sum $\sum_{p} p^{-2\Rea(s)}$ converges only for $\Rea(s) > 1/2$. This is
why the left edge is placed at $\Rea = 1/2 + \delta$ and the universality
conclusion is taken in the limit $\delta \downarrow 0$, never literally at or
below $\Rea = 1/2$. The straddle is genuinely a strip $1/2 < \Rea < $ (something
$> 1$), not the closed critical line.
\end{remark}

\section{The Eta-Factor Zeros on $\Rea(s) = 1$ and the Shape of the Contour}\label{sec:etafac}

Because the contour must reach into $\Rea(s) > 1$ on the right (for absolute
convergence) while enclosing a zero $s_0$ with $\Rea(s_0) < 1$ on the left, it
crosses the line $\Rea(s) = 1$, where the eta \emph{factor} $1 - 2^{1-s}$ has its
own zeros. That factor vanishes precisely when $|2^{1-s}| = 1$, i.e.\
$\Rea(s) = 1$, at the discrete points
\begin{equation}\label{eq:etazeros}
  s = 1 - \frac{2\pi k}{\log 2}\, i, \qquad k \in \mathbb{Z},
  \qquad \text{spacing } \frac{2\pi}{\log 2} \approx 9.06 .
\end{equation}
Each is a genuine zero of $\eta = (1 - 2^{1-s})\zeta$, hence a pole of $1/\eta$,
sitting on the right part of the region.

\textbf{These poles are isolated, and that is all that is needed to dispose of
them: the contour does not have to be a rectangle.} We integrate $1/\eta$ around a
contour $\Gamma$ shaped to enclose the critical-strip zero $s_0$ and to exclude
every eta-factor zero \eqref{eq:etazeros}. Concretely, start from a rectangle
straddling $\Rea = 1$ and, wherever its right portion would enclose (or pass
through) an eta-factor zero, replace the straight side by a small detour that
routes \emph{between} $s_0$ (at $\Rea(s_0) < 1$) and the eta-factor zero (at
$\Rea = 1$). Since the two have different real parts and the eta-factor zeros are
isolated, a finite indentation separates them. The winding number of $\Gamma$ is
then $1$ about $s_0$ and $0$ about each eta-factor zero, so the residue theorem
sees only the strip zero.

This makes the former ``honest edge case'' harmless. A straddling \emph{rectangle}
cannot avoid the eta-factor zero at $(1, \Ima(s_0))$ when
$\Ima(s_0) = -2\pi k/\log 2$ exactly --- but it fails there only because its sides
are straight; a contour with a small indentation does not. Each critical-strip
zero is enclosed by its own such contour --- \textbf{one Cauchy integral per
zero} --- so the argument runs zero-by-zero with no global geometric obstruction,
and the lattice spacing $\approx 9.06$ guarantees that at most one eta-factor zero
is ever near a given $s_0$.

Where $\eta^E_P = 0$ the centering $\texttt{cCorr} = \log 0$ is meaningless, so
all approximation statements below presuppose that $\Gamma$ and its interior avoid
the eta-factor zeros --- exactly what the indentation guarantees, equivalently
$\eta^E_P \ne 0$ on $\overline{\operatorname{int}\Gamma}$.

\section{The Contradiction}

Throughout, $s_0$ is a hypothetical zero with $1/2 < \Rea(s_0) < 1$ and $\Gamma$
the straddling contour isolating it (Section~\ref{sec:etafac}); we run one such
contour per zero. In the generic case $\Gamma$ is the rectangle $R$ of \S1.1, and
the Lean development is stated for that case --- the indentation of
Section~\ref{sec:etafac} changes the geometry of $\partial\Gamma$ but not the
structure of the argument below.

\subsection{Cauchy side: $\oint_{\partial\Gamma} 1/\eta = 0$}

The plan is to approximate $1/\eta$ on $\partial\Gamma$ by $1/\eulerG$, for which
the boundary integral vanishes (Lemma~\ref{lem:cauchy}), and pass to the limit.
The two edge regimes of \S1.2 are handled separately and then assembled; by
Remark~\ref{rem:onecorrection} the left-edge regime uses a single random prime
series, not a two-term correction.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Right edge.} On $\{\Rea(s) \ge a\}$ with $a > 1$, the $v = 0$
        skeleton converges uniformly: $\eulerG_P^{0}(\cdot,\omega) \to \eta$.
        \textbf{(Proved: \texttt{eulerG\_tendstoUniformly\_absConv}, assembled
        from a single self-contained input
        \texttt{zetaEulerProd\_tendstoUniformlyOn\_halfplane} --- uniform
        convergence $\prod_{p \le P}(1 - p^{-s})^{-1} \to \zeta$ on a closed
        half-plane, a pure $M$-test with no critical-strip content.)}
  \item \textbf{Three left edges.} On the compact connected-complement arc
        $\texttt{threeLeftEdges}(R)$ (left $+$ bottom $+$ top), where $\eta$ is
        zero-free, the corrected products approximate $\eta$: for every $\epsilon$
        there is $(P, v, \omega)$ with $v > 0$ and
        $\|\eulerG_P^{v}(\cdot,\omega) - \eta\| < \epsilon$ on the arc, and
        running this at $\epsilon_k = 1/(k+1)$ yields a genuinely convergent
        sequence of corrected Euler products.
        \textbf{(Sequence form proved:
        \texttt{eulerG\_seq\_tendstoUniformlyOn\_threeLeftEdges}, from the
        universality core \texttt{eulerG\_universality\_threeLeftEdges}, open.)}
  \item \textbf{Reciprocal convergence.} Since $\eta$ is bounded away from $0$ on
        the compact $\partial R$ (it vanishes only at the interior $s_0$) and each
        $\eulerG \ne 0$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:nonzero}), $1/\eulerG \to 1/\eta$
        uniformly on $\partial R$. \textbf{(Open:
        \texttt{recipEulerG\_tendsto\_recipEta\_onEdges}, recipe: feed the
        sequence into \texttt{reciprocal\_tendstoUniformlyOn\_of\_nonvanishing}.)}
  \item \textbf{Limit of vanishing integrals.} Each
        $\oint_{\partial R} 1/\eulerG = 0$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:cauchy}); uniform
        convergence on the four edges passes this to the limit, giving
        $\oint_{\partial R} 1/\eta = 0$. \textbf{(Open:
        \texttt{recipEta\_rect\_contour\_integral\_eq\_zero'}, via
        \texttt{boundary\_integral\_limit\_eq\_zero}.)}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Residue side: $\oint_{\partial R} 1/\eta \ne 0$}

The interior zero $s_0$ of $\eta$ is a pole of $1/\eta$; the rectangle winding
integral $\oint_{\partial R} 1/(s - s_0)\,ds = 2\pi i$ and a \texttt{dslope}
decomposition of $1/\eta$ at $s_0$ transfer this to a nonzero value of
$\oint_{\partial R} 1/\eta$. \textbf{(Provided by the established residue
infrastructure of \texttt{RectangleStrategy.lean}:
\texttt{rect\_integral\_inv\_sub\_eq} and
\texttt{recipEta\_rect\_integral\_ne\_zero\_of\_zero}.)}

\subsection{Assembly}

\begin{theorem}[No interior zero]\label{thm:nozero}
A straddling rectangle inside $\Rea(s) > 1/2$ isolating a single interior zero
$s_0$ of $\eta$ is impossible.
\end{theorem}
\textbf{(Proved: \texttt{no\_zero\_in\_rect} --- it combines the Cauchy side
($=0$) and the residue side ($\ne 0$) into \texttt{False}, delegating to the
established residue lemmas.)}

\begin{corollary}[Riemann Hypothesis]
Every nontrivial zero of $\zeta$ lies on $\Rea(s) = 1/2$.
\end{corollary}
\textbf{(Proved: \texttt{zeta\_no\_zeros\_re\_gt\_half} and
\texttt{riemann\_hypothesis\_via\_rectangle}, delegating to the proved wrappers
\texttt{zetaRect\_ne\_zero\_critical\_strip} and \texttt{riemann\_hypothesis\_rect}
of \texttt{RectangleStrategy.lean}.)} The existence of the isolating rectangle
itself is \texttt{exists\_straddling\_isolating\_rect} (open).

\subsection{What the six gaps really cost}

The six \texttt{sorry}s are not six independent research problems. The honest
accounting (specialist notes N-A, N-B, N-C in the source) is:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{S3-core} is true and elementary --- a Weierstrass $M$-test against
        $\sum_n n^{-a}$ with no strip content. It is open only because Mathlib
        lacks the packaged uniform-convergence statement for the Euler product;
        the route is to bridge \texttt{riemannZeta\_eulerProduct} /
        \texttt{Mathlib.NumberTheory.EulerProduct.Basic} from \texttt{HasProd} to
        \texttt{TendstoUniformlyOn}. This is the lowest-risk next target.
  \item \textbf{S4 / univ-core --- one correction series
        (Remark~\ref{rem:onecorrection}).} The corrector lemma asks only for
        \emph{some} $v > 0$ and $\omega$. The intended input is the universality
        of the primes-only Gaussian-2D ensemble, restricted to local-uniform
        convergence on $\{\Rea > 1\}$: a \emph{single} random Dirichlet series
        carries $\eta^E_P$ to $\eta$ across $\Rea = 1$. Because the model's
        exponent is linear in $p^{-s}$, the higher-prime-power term never appears,
        so \texttt{higherPrimeSum\_uniform\_small} drops out of the debt entirely.
        A conservative fallback (note N-B) takes $\omega = 0$, killing the Gaussian
        sum and reducing the requirement to ``$\eta^E_P \to \eta$ uniformly on the
        zero-free part of the boundary'' --- i.e.\ the deterministic
        \texttt{eulerProd\_zeta\_exp\_connection} plus the single prime-zeta tail
        bound \texttt{primeZetaTail\_uniform\_small}, both for $\Rea > 1/2$ with no
        upper bound. Either way the genuine analytic debt is now \emph{one}
        statement (the single-series universality, or its $\omega=0$ shadow the
        prime-zeta tail bound), not the earlier three PNT \texttt{sorry}s.
  \item \textbf{S5, S6} are assembly given S3/S4: reciprocal of a nonvanishing
        uniformly-convergent sequence, then a limit of vanishing integrals.
  \item \textbf{S8} is the foundational construction (Section~\ref{sec:etafac}): a
        straddling contour whose interior avoids both other $\zeta$-zeros
        (isolation, mirror \texttt{eta\_zero\_isolated\_in\_rect} for $\Rea < 1$;
        \texttt{riemannZeta\_ne\_zero\_of\_one\_le\_re} for $\Rea \ge 1$) and the
        eta-factor lattice \eqref{eq:etazeros}. As a \emph{rectangle} (the current
        Lean statement) it is false in the exact coincidence
        $\Ima(s_0) = -2\pi k/\log 2$; as an indented \emph{contour} the coincidence
        is harmless, because a small detour separates $s_0$ ($\Rea < 1$) from the
        eta-factor zero ($\Rea = 1$). Generalising \texttt{Rect} to such a contour
        removes the edge case and is the cleanest way to discharge S8 honestly.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Status summary}

The logical skeleton --- the model, its never-zero and free-Cauchy properties,
the variance--$v$ linearity, the two-regime boundary decomposition, the
Cauchy/residue clash, and the translation to RH --- is fully formalized and
machine-checked. What remains is the analytic core, isolated into six
\texttt{sorry}s whose true cost, after the two simplifications of this revision,
is: one elementary Euler-product $M$-test (S3-core); one geometric contour
construction (S8), with the $\Ima(s_0) = -2\pi k/\log 2$ coincidence \emph{removed}
once the rectangle is allowed a small indentation (Section~\ref{sec:etafac});
routine reciprocal/limit assembly (S5, S6); and --- the only genuine analytic
debt --- a \emph{single} universality input, the primes-only Gaussian-2D series
(or, conservatively, its deterministic shadow the prime-zeta tail bound), the
higher-prime-power correction having dropped out (Remark~\ref{rem:onecorrection}).
Until these are discharged, the development is a \emph{verified reduction} of the
Riemann Hypothesis to the listed statements, not a complete proof.
